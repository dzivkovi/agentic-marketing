I'm an enterprise AI architect who accidentally spent a decade doing marketing. This project exists because those two threads finally converged.

## The Origin Story

### Fishing

I discovered fishing around 2001. The dot-com era had me chained to a desk - long hours, junk food, rising cholesterol. I needed something to physically disconnect from the computer. Fishing became that escape - and then became an obsession. Early mornings, cold rain, muddy banks. For the next six years, every free weekend I had went to the water.

The thing nobody tells you about fishing is that it's mostly problem-solving: reading the water, choosing the right bait, finding where the fish are before you cast. You can't force a bite. You present the right thing at the right time, and you wait.

The question that drives every fishing trip is the same one that drives every marketing campaign: **"What do they bite on?"**

### Real Estate Marketing (2005-2015)

Around 2005, my wife Jasmina became a real estate agent in Toronto. I became her marketing department: SEO, content, email campaigns, SMS, and open houses. I even got my real estate license so I could work the open houses legally and engage with prospects directly.

I learned fast. My first lesson was about targeting. I advertised Jasmina as a "Greater Toronto Area" real estate agent - five million people, zero leads. When I narrowed to "Richmond Hill Real Estate" and "North York Condos," leads started flowing. She ended up selling outside those areas anyway. Sharper the hook, better the catch.

My second lesson was about patience. In fishing, if you pull the line too fast, the fish releases the bait before the hook sets. In real estate, jumping on a lead too aggressively signals desperation and breaks the relationship before it forms. The prospect needs time to move from awareness to trust - the same progression that Gary Vaynerchuk captures as "Jab, Jab, Jab, Right Hook" (give, give, give, then ask) and Dean Jackson describes through his lead nurturing philosophy. I discovered these principles empirically before I knew they had names.

I was never the closer. Jasmina is patient, nurturing, an introvert who builds deep trust. I'm loud, fast, extroverted - good at attracting attention, not great at sitting with it. So I built the system: the SEO that got her found on Google, the email sequences that kept leads warm, open house signs with IVR and SMS response codes that captured leads before they walked through the door, and the analytics that told us what was working. She closed the deals.

When I earned my **Inbound Marketing University** certification in 2009 (signed by Brian Halligan, HubSpot's CEO), it was the first time I realized the patterns I'd discovered through fishing and real estate had an entire industry behind them.

### The Hidden Marketer

For years, I hid all of this from my IT career. The tech industry in the 2000s didn't reward outside interests. I watched a colleague at Sun Microsystems - a talented engineer who happened to keep horses - get cut first when downsizing came. The unspoken rule was clear: if you had passions outside of IT, you weren't serious enough. So "real estate marketing" stayed a private hobby, never a resume line.

What I didn't realize was that the marketing work was teaching me something my IT peers weren't learning: how to think about the person on the other end of the system. Not the user in the UX sense - the *customer*. What makes them act. What makes them trust. What makes them choose you over the other option. That understanding would become the thing that separated me from every other architect in the room, but it took another decade to surface.

### Enterprise Marketing Technology (2008-2017)

The enterprise world pulled me in, and it kept putting me at the intersection of marketing and IT:

**2008-2009** - My first attempt at running my own business. I took on Baxter Media Publishing (known for their travel industry magazines) as a client, modernizing their print-to-digital publishing workflows, introducing web content management, SEO, and asset management. I was also doing real estate marketing technology - Drupal websites, Twilio for lead tracking and call recording (which earned me Twilio Champion status, their developer ambassador program). The business didn't scale; I couldn't compete on price with offshore development shops, and I retreated back to enterprise contracts. But the marketing technology instinct was now permanent.

**HOOPP, 2010-2014** - As Business Systems Analyst and Solutions Architect, I built marketing technology for Ontario's healthcare pension plan. From CMS vendor selection to website redesign with deliberate SEO ranking preservation, CRM and email marketing platform evaluation, to SharePoint content management migration. The director of marketing taught me the lesson that became Commandment #2 in this project's framework: *"Daniel, show me what you have and I'll tell you what I want."* Marketers don't produce requirements from a blank page. They need a catalyst to react to.

**Tangerine Bank, 2014-2015** - After Tangerine's independence from ING, I helped unify their two disparate web properties (the main banking site and their blog site) into a single content management platform, with lead scoring and audience segmentation built into the architecture from day one. I pushed for SEO best practices, helped migrate their marketing automation to improve lead nurturing, and worked on real-time personalization using customer behavioral data - converting anonymous web visitors into known prospects.

The senior manager of content and social media marketing on that project later wrote:

> *"I'd like to highlight Daniel's 'superpower' - his ability to bridge the conversations between the 'business' and 'IT'. In fact I described him then as a 'unicorn' and he remains one of the few IT professionals I've worked with who have an uncanny ability to translate business needs, shield us from the complexity of the back end, and work to provide solutions that benefit all stakeholders."*

That word - "unicorn" - stuck. Not because it's flattering, but because it named something I'd been doing without a label: sitting in both rooms, understanding both languages, building the bridge that neither side could build alone.

### Cloud, AI, and the Pivot (2017-present)

The marketing thread went quiet as I dove into cloud computing and AI:

- **Serverless computing and cloud-native architecture** - I founded [Serverless Toronto](https://youtube.serverlesstoronto.org/), a community of 5,200+ members. Tagline: "Bridging the gap between IT and business needs." (My wife vetoed the original: "Home of Less IT Mess." It's still on the YouTube channel.)
- **Google Cloud** - Professional Data Engineer and Cloud Solutions Architect certifications. Professional services projects through Google Cloud PSO partners for Ford, Activision Blizzard, Indiana University Health.
- **AI and ML** - Eight years of applied machine learning across healthcare, retail, financial services, and supply chain.
- **Generative AI** - When GPT launched in late 2022, I went head first. Built a stealth AI automation agency (it didn't work as a business, but it built the skills). That led to Nasdaq hiring me as an AI Engineer (13 months building document intelligence pipelines and LLM observability). Then Siemens (a year on large codebase analysis, which became a product specialization at [magmainc.ca](https://magmainc.ca)).

### The Convergence

Two seemingly unconnectable threads - a decade of marketing technology and a decade of enterprise AI - are now converging. AI makes marketing systematizable in ways it never was before. The patterns I learned from fishing (read the water, match the bait, be patient), from real estate (specificity beats reach, give before you ask), and from enterprise IT (build the bridge, show don't tell, catalyst not blank page) all apply directly to the problem of introducing agentic AI into marketing organizations.

In 2026, I began helping a major global payments company introduce agentic AI into their marketing organization. It wasn't a pivot - it was a convergence. Two decades of marketing instinct and enterprise architecture finally had the same address. This framework is what came out of it.

## The Influences

My marketing education was self-directed, traced back through primary sources. When someone taught me something, I looked for their teachers:

- **Dan Kennedy** - direct-response marketing pioneer. His "No B.S." book series and "Magnetic Marketing" system are the root curriculum. Most of the people below learned from him. His core principle: results rule, period. Track everything, measure everything, never spend money on "brand building" when you can buy measurable response instead
- **Dean Jackson** - lead nurturing, patience, the art of not spooking the prospect. His insight that jumping on leads immediately signals desperation shaped how I thought about email sequences and follow-up timing
- **Joe Polish** - connection-first marketing, relationship capital. The idea that your network of genuine relationships is your most valuable business asset
- **Gary Vaynerchuk** - "Jab, Jab, Jab, Right Hook" (give, give, give, then ask). Content as generosity. The framework I use to think about how many times I've given value before asking for anything
- **Jay Abraham** - leverage, strategic alliances, joint ventures. His book "Getting Everything You Can Out of All You've Got" reframed how I think about business growth: there are only three ways to grow (more clients, bigger transactions, more frequent purchases). Everything else is a tactic serving one of those three
- **David Ogilvy** - copywriting craft, research-driven creative, respect for the reader. When I had to write everything manually for real estate, his books taught me that good copy is built on research, not inspiration

On the engineering side:

- **Google's Design Thinking** (the Double Diamond) - the operating model for discovery and delivery
- **Systems thinking** - applied across disciplines from requirements analysis to architecture. The discipline of seeing the whole system before optimizing the parts
- **Andrej Karpathy's Software 3.0** - the framing that English is the new programming language, and SOPs are executable AI infrastructure
- **Anthropic Agent Skills specification** - the portable unit of work that makes agentic marketing vendor-agnostic
